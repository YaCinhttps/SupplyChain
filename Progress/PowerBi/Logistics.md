## What This Page Answers

This page puts the operational health of the business under a microscope. It moves beyond revenue and profit to ask a harder question — **are we actually delivering on our promises to customers?** The answer, across every market, region, and shipping mode, is that we are not.

---

## The Story This Page Tells

**More than half of all orders arrive late — and the cause is not what you'd expect.**

The headline number on this page is impossible to ignore: **54.82% of all orders are delivered late.** That means for every 2 customers who place an order, more than 1 receives it after the promised date. This is not a regional problem, not a seasonal blip, and not the result of one underperforming carrier. It is a systemic operational failure that runs across every market and every region in the dataset.

But the most important finding on this page isn't the overall late rate — it's **where the problem actually comes from.**

**First Class shipping is failing customers 95.27% of the time.** Customers who pay a premium for expedited delivery are receiving their orders late on virtually every single order. The root cause is not a logistics breakdown — it is a scheduling misconfiguration. First Class promises delivery in 1 day but consistently takes 2. Second Class promises 2 days and consistently takes 4. In both cases, the scheduled delivery estimate was set one day too optimistic across the board, making every order technically late before it even leaves the warehouse.

The Average Days by Shipping Mode chart confirms this precisely. First Class: promised 1 day, actual 2 days. Second Class: promised 2 days, actual 4 days. Standard Class: promised 4 days, actual 4 days — the only mode that meets its commitment. Same Day: promised 0 days, actual 0.48 days — reasonable given the nature of same-day logistics.

**This means the fix is not a logistics overhaul — it is a data correction.** Updating the scheduled delivery estimates for First Class from 1 day to 2 days, and for Second Class from 2 days to 4 days, would bring both modes into alignment with reality and dramatically improve the reported on-time rate overnight. No operational change required.

**The financial cost of late delivery is real but smaller than expected.** The Profit Margin donut chart shows that on-time orders carry a marginally higher margin than late orders — but the gap is narrow. The more significant cost is the reputational and retention impact, which this dataset does not directly measure but can be inferred: customers who pay for First Class and receive their order a day late are unlikely to choose that option again.

**Geography tells the same story everywhere.** The Late Rate by State map shows that late deliveries are distributed broadly — no single country or region stands out as a dramatic outlier. When the map is filtered by country, the pattern holds: the late delivery problem is not concentrated in one place. This is further confirmation that the issue lives in the scheduling logic, not in the physical logistics network.

**Only 2.08% of orders are canceled** — a relatively controlled figure across all markets, with no single region showing a standout cancellation problem.

The single most actionable takeaway from this page: **correct the scheduled delivery day estimates for First Class and Second Class shipping modes.** This one change, which requires no operational investment, would transform the reported delivery performance of the business overnight and restore customer trust in premium shipping options.
